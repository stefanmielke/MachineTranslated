# Chapter 12.5: Daon

<b>

<b>

<b>

Daon had become the head of Vitt Village five years ago. He had originally been a holy knight who hunted monsters, but retired at thirty-five and returned to his home village, where his parents, wanting a quiet retirement, handed the village chief duties to him. The villagers didn’t seem to object to a former holy knight like Daon becoming their head.

Vitt Village sits beside a certain mountain range. It’s known as the dragon’s deathland, and over five hundred years the miasma has been thinning—about the time when a monster domain begins to form.

<b>

(You can tell people to leave because it’s dangerous, but everyone still resists abandoning their homeland… Honestly, stubborn as they are.)

<b>

You never know what kind of monsters will arise in a monster domain. It would be preferable if stationary plant-type monsters built an ecosystem, but if orcs that snatch people or carnivorous monsters that eat any species spread down the mountain, Vitt Village would become extremely dangerous.

<b>

But if that were to happen, Daon returned here to protect his home and people. That must be why the villagers welcomed him as their chief.

<b>

<b>

“Uncle Daon, something’s…wrong…”

<b>

“Ellie? Hey, are you all right!?”

<b>

<b>

Ellie, a girl who lived next door, was very fond of Daon. That day she’d been eating sweets and drinking tea at his house, but suddenly went pale and vomited what she’d eaten.

Children falling ill wasn’t unheard of. But Ellie wasn’t the only one to lose their appetite that day. They couldn’t eat at all. Even water they drank mostly came back up. In three days the villagers could barely move and were stuck inside their homes.

<b>

(What is this illness… The rate at which it’s spreading is strange. Is it the work of monsters?)

<b>

Perhaps a highly toxic monster was lurking nearby, harming the villagers. By the time he reached that thought, Daon had collapsed with the same symptoms.

He drank the water stored in his jug and vomited it back up. But if he didn’t take even tiny amounts of fluid that way, he would shrivel up and die.

He’d been prepared to fight monsters. Even as he aged he had trained his body every day to guard against monster attacks. He’d never imagined it would come in a form like this—

<b>

(…? …The door opened…?)

<b>

There was a sound from the front entrance. Someone entered the house, their footsteps clicking on the floor. Immediately a fragrant floral scent began to fill the room.

It wasn’t a villager. From his bed, gripping a dagger, he watched the figure advancing toward him.

<b>

She was a very beautiful woman. Her features were so perfectly arranged she looked like a painting or sculpture by a famed artist—crafted to perfection. She was adorned with all kinds of flowers. The scent came from those blooms.

<b>

—Who the hell are you. How did you get in here.

<b>

He wanted to say that, but his parched throat stuck and the words wouldn’t form.

She had entered through the front door he’d thought locked, so this woman was no ordinary person. Her manufactured beauty made her eerie.

Even when he aimed his killing intent at her as a warning, she didn’t seem to mind at all; she only smiled more deeply. For a seasoned holy knight who’d dealt with countless monsters to have his intent ignored like that—what on earth was this woman?

<b>

(…Damn, no matter what she does I can’t win against this…)

<b>

He tried to gather strength in the hand holding his sword, but his body wouldn’t respond. His internal moisture was drained so he didn’t sweat, but he was breaking out in a cold sweat inside.

Yet the woman did nothing to him; she turned her back and left. Just as he felt deflated, the door opened again. This time the woman returned not alone but holding hands with two children. One of them was the familiar girl.

<b>

<b>

“Ellie…?”

<b>

<b>

The last time he’d seen Ellie she could only retch in agony, but now she appeared lively with no sign of illness. Surprised, he watched as she offered what looked like a fruit very similar to those from the will-o’-wisp plant.

It seemed to be a medicine the woman—the witch—had prepared. If Ellie had recovered this much, it might indeed be effective.

<b>

(…Well, at this point I’ll probably just die anyway…)

<b>

It wasn’t a bad idea to try it as a last resort. He bit into the fruit and swallowed the liquid inside. The taste was one he’d had once before. It was a complete recovery medicine.

Who would generously offer such a valuable potion to a total stranger? And that such a person would appear in a village on the brink of death—what a miracle.

<b>

<b>

“Uncle, we’ll go help the others too!”

<b>

“…Yeah, you go.”

<b>

<b>

If she could save the villagers, then whatever scheme she had in mind could be set aside—lives came first.

Daon nodded at the witch, meaning “please,” and the witch smiled gently and left his house. That day, all the villagers were saved by the witch’s complete recovery potion. …Was he dreaming? It was all too unreal.

<b>

(…Who on earth is that witch, and what’s her purpose?)

<b>

The last survivor of the demon race was said to be a man. Not a witch but a sorcerer, employed by the nation as a court mage. Daon, who had been a holy knight, had seen that court mage before and knew the witch who saved the village today was a completely different person.

<b>

(They said all the demons were wiped out except one man, but could there be other survivors? And she’s been hiding from humans for five hundred years?)

<b>

The witch saved more than fifty villagers with her complete recovery medicine. Her kindness was excessive—too much kindness can itself look suspicious.

<b>

(I should test her a little… She might be up to something.)

<b>

It was a natural judgement for a village head responsible for protecting the people. Still—

Watching the beautiful woman smile serenely at the villagers’ gratitude, taking the hands of the two lively children and being fussed over by them while she laughed gently, it was hard to want to suspect her heart.

<b>

(…If I knew anything about painting, I’d probably be painting this scene…)

<b>

The villagers gazed at the woman who’d saved them with great medicine, not demanding thanks but playing with the children she’d healed—mixing gratitude and respect with warm amusement. That sight was more dazzling than the high sun.

<b>

Children are sensitive to malice. Ellie in particular had once approached someone who looked friendly when kidnappers had appeared in the village, but she’d been wary and fled—and come to Daon for help.

Despite his rough, intimidating appearance, she was very attached to Daon. He’d always thought she had an eye for judging right and wrong. If Ellie adored that witch so much, could that witch really harbor ill intent?

<b>

(I don’t like it… but if this suspicion is false, then at the very least I should be able to do something to make amends—something to help that woman…)
